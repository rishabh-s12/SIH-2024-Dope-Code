Types of Doping Substances

Anabolic Steroids: Synthetic variations of testosterone used to increase muscle mass and strength (e.g., nandrolone, stanozolol).

Erythropoietin (EPO): A hormone that increases red blood cell production, enhancing endurance.

Stimulants: Substances like amphetamines and ephedrine increase alertness, energy, and competitiveness.

Human Growth Hormone (HGH): Used to increase muscle mass and reduce body fat.

Blood Doping: Involves transfusing extra blood to increase oxygen delivery to muscles.

Diuretics: Used to lose weight quickly or mask other drugs.

Common Methods of Doping

  • Oral Intake: Pills or capsules containing banned substances.
  • Injections: Directly administering substances into the bloodstream or muscles.
  • Inhalation: Some drugs, especially stimulants, can be inhaled.
  • Blood Transfusions: Transferring blood to increase red blood cell count.
  • Masking Agents: Substances used to hide or interfere with the detection of doping substances.

Health Risks of Doping

Cardiovascular Issues: Increased risk of heart attack, stroke, and blood clots.

Hormonal Imbalances: Steroids and HGH can lead to infertility, aggression, and liver damage.

Psychological Effects: Stimulants and steroids can cause anxiety, depression, and aggression.

Infection Risk: Improper injection techniques can lead to infections like HIV.

Addiction: Stimulants can cause physical and psychological dependence.

Consequences of Doping

Disqualification and Stripping of Titles: Athletes caught doping often lose their titles, medals, and records.

Bans and Suspensions: Athletes may face multi-year or life bans from competitions.

Reputational Damage: Doping scandals can damage an athlete's reputation and lead to loss of sponsorships.

Legal Consequences: Doping may result in fines or imprisonment in some countries.

Process of Doping

Selection of Substances or Methods: Athletes or support teams select performance-enhancing drugs or methods.

Administration: PEDs are taken orally, injected, or through blood transfusions.

Concealment: Athletes may use masking agents or microdosing to avoid detection.

Testing and Detection: Drug tests such as urine and blood tests are used to detect banned substances.

Consequences: Athletes caught doping may face disqualification, suspension, and reputational damage.
